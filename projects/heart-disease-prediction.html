Heart Disease Prediction Using Machine Learning

Leveraging AI to Enable Early Detection and Save Lives

Problem Statement

According to the World Health Organization, heart disease is the leading cause of death worldwide, claiming over 17 million lives each year. In South Africa, the burden of heart-related illnesses continues to rise due to lifestyle factors, limited access to regular screenings, and late diagnosis. Traditional diagnostic methods such as ECGs, blood tests, and stress tests are effective but often time-consuming, costly, and not easily accessible in resource-limited settings. This project applies machine learning to predict heart disease risk early, helping improve access to timely diagnosis and preventive care.

How It Works

This AI-powered heart disease prediction system uses machine learning classification algorithms to analyze 13 critical health indicators and predict the likelihood of heart disease with over 90% accuracy, making early detection accessible to healthcare facilities worldwide.

Development Process

1. Data Collection & Understanding

Acquired comprehensive heart disease dataset with 1,000+ patient records containing 13 health indicators. Performed initial exploration to understand data structure, types, and distributions.

2. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Created comprehensive visualizations including heart disease distribution, age patterns, correlation heatmaps, and comparative analyses of cholesterol, blood pressure, heart rate, and ST depression across disease status. Saved all plots for documentation.

3. Data Preprocessing & Feature Engineering

Separated features from target variable, performed train-test split (80-20) with stratification to maintain class balance, and standardized all numerical features using StandardScaler for optimal model performance.

4. Model Training & Comparison

Trained and evaluated multiple algorithms: Logistic Regression, Random Forest (200 estimators), SVM with RBF kernel, and K-Nearest Neighbors. Systematically compared accuracy scores and classification reports to identify the best performer.

5. Model Evaluation & Selection

Random Forest emerged as the best model with 90%+ accuracy. Generated detailed classification reports and confusion matrix to validate performance. Created model comparison visualization showing accuracy across all algorithms.

6. Model Persistence

Saved the trained Random Forest model using joblib for deployment. Exported model columns to JSON file to ensure correct feature alignment during prediction in the web application.

7. Streamlit Web Application Development

Built interactive web application in PyCharm with multiple pages: Home, Predict (input form with 13 health indicators), Model Insights (feature importance visualization), and About. Implemented probability scoring and risk classification.

8. Deployment & Testing

Deployed the Streamlit application for real-world testing. Validated predictions with various patient profiles to ensure consistent and accurate risk assessments across different health indicator combinations.

Key Data Insights

Through comprehensive exploratory data analysis, I uncovered critical patterns that inform the model's predictions:

Heart Disease Distribution

The dataset shows a balanced distribution between patients with and without heart disease, with approximately equal representation of both classes. This balanced distribution is crucial for training an unbiased machine learning model that can accurately predict both positive and negative cases.

Age Distribution

The age distribution follows a normal distribution pattern, with most patients falling in the middle-age range. The data shows that the majority of patients in the dataset are between 45-65 years old, which aligns with the typical age range for cardiovascular disease risk assessment.

Age vs Heart Disease

This boxplot reveals that patients with heart disease tend to be slightly older on average compared to those without the condition. The median age for heart disease patients is higher, and there is a clear trend showing increased heart disease prevalence with advancing age, particularly in the 55+ age group.

Cholesterol Levels vs Heart Disease

This boxplot shows that cholesterol levels among patients with heart disease are generally more variable compared to those without the condition. While the median cholesterol values are fairly similar between the two groups, heart disease patients display a wider range and more extreme values, indicating that high cholesterol may be a contributing factor in certain cases. The presence of several outliers also suggests that abnormally high cholesterol levels occur more frequently in patients with heart disease.

Resting Blood Pressure vs Heart Disease

his boxplot indicates that patients with heart disease generally have slightly higher resting systolic blood pressure compared to those without the condition. The heart disease group also shows greater variability, suggesting that fluctuations or elevated blood pressure levels may be more common among them. Although the difference in median values is not large, the wider spread and presence of outliers in the heart disease group may reflect a link between irregular blood pressure and heart disease risk.

Maximum Heart Rate vs Heart Disease

Maximum heart rate achieved during exercise testing reveals important patterns. Patients with heart disease generally show lower maximum heart rates compared to healthy individuals, indicating reduced cardiovascular capacity and exercise tolerance. This metric is a strong predictor in the model.

ST Depression (Oldpeak) vs Heart Disease

This boxplot reveals that patients with heart disease tend to have higher Oldpeak values compared to those without the condition. The median Oldpeak and overall variability are greater among heart disease patients, indicating more pronounced ST depression during exercise, a common sign of reduced heart function or ischemia. The presence of several high outliers further supports the link between elevated Oldpeak levels and heart disease. This is one of the most significant predictors in the model.

Feature Correlation Heatmap

This feature correlation heatmap shows how different health factors relate to each other in a heart disease prediction project. Most features have weak correlations, meaning they are mostly independent and suitable for machine learning. Age is negatively correlated with maximum heart rate (-0.37), showing that older people tend to have lower heart rates. Chest pain type, ST slope, and exercise angina are more strongly related to the target variable, suggesting they play an important role in predicting heart disease. In contrast, cholesterol, resting blood pressure, and fasting blood sugar show weak relationships and may be less useful for prediction.

Model Accuracy Comparison

This bar chart compares the performance of four different machine learning algorithms tested on the heart disease dataset. Random Forest achieved the highest accuracy at over 90%, outperforming Logistic Regression, SVM, and KNN. This systematic comparison justified the selection of Random Forest as the final model.

Confusion Matrix - Random Forest

The confusion matrix provides detailed insight into the model's prediction performance, showing true positives, true negatives, false positives, and false negatives. The high values along the diagonal indicate strong classification accuracy, with minimal misclassifications demonstrating the model's reliability for clinical use.
